-the small, precise, or trivial details of a fingerprint.

-a supposition or proposed explanation made on the basis of limited evidence as a starting point for further investigation.

-a variable that is not affected by other variables in a study and is often manipulated by the researcher

 -What you measure in the experiment and what is affected during the experiment

Blood typing

  • Antibodies:  Proteins produced by the immune system when it detects harmful substances (Antigens)

Red blood cells

  • A type of blood cell that is made in the bone marrow and found in the blood. Red blood cells carry oxygen from the lungs and deliver it throughout our body

White blood cells

-Part of the body’s immune system, and its function is to protect your body from infection

Platelets

-Small, colorless cell fragments in our blood that form clots to stop and prevent bleeding

Plasma

-Takes the role of taking nutrients, hormones, and proteins to the parts of the body that needs it. It is a state of matter along with solids, liquids, and gasses

  • Platelets: forms clots to prevent bleeding.

  • White blood cell: protects the body from infection

Agglutination

-The blood has reacted with a certain antibody and is not compatible with blood containing that type of antibody. The blood clumps together.

Nitrogen bases (how they pair)

  • There are four Nitrogen bases: Adenine, Thymine, Cytosine, and Guanine.
  • Two distinct groups:
  • Purines: Adenine, Guanine.
  • Pyrimidines: Cytosine, Thymine
  •  The bases form hydrogen bonds with one another and pair. Adenine is paired with Thymine, and Cytosine pairs with Guanine
  • Adenine has two hydrogen bonds with Thymine
  • Cytosine has three hydrogen bonds with Guanine.

  • Covalent bond (C): Involves shared electron pairs between atoms
  • Hydrogen bonds: An attraction between 2 atoms that already participate in other chemical bonds

Phosphate groups (black circle):

  • Adenine, Thymine, Cytosine, Guanine

Deoxyribose sugar (blue pentagon):

  • Sugar in the phosphate backbone of DNA

Nitrogenous base:

  • a molecule that contains nitrogen and has the chemical properties of a base

Eukaryotic cell: 

- any cell with a nucleus

Prokaryotic cell:

- any cell without a nucleus

Organelles: 

- Subcellular structure that has one or more specific jobs to perform in the cell

Nucleus

- The nucleus is the cell's control center and repository of genetic information. DNA replication, transcription, and RNA processing all take place in the nucleus.

 DNA

-Deoxyribonucleic Acid (DNA) is the code of an instruction manual for creating all the necessary proteins that our body needs to live.

Chromosomes:

- A package of DNA that contains all of the genetic information of an organism wrapped in an X and Y shape.

Polymerase Chain Reaction (PCR):

-A laboratory technique for rapidly reproducing millions of copies of a specific segment of DNA.

Restriction Enzyme:

- A specific type of enzyme that splits DNA segments in a specific area, causing DNA fragments to form.

Recognition Site:

-The place where the restriction enzyme is used to split DNA in half.

Restriction Digest:

-A procedure that prepares DNA to be analyzed or used in other processing.

RFLPs:

-Restriction Fragment Length Polymorphisms (RFLPs) are genetic markers that are often used to follow the inheritance of DNA through families.

Gel electrophoresis (what is it and how does it work):

-Laboratory methods used to separate mixtures of DNA, RNA, and proteins according to size.

-Molecules are separated by an electric current while being in distilled water. The molecules are attracted towards the positive charge since DNA is negatively charged.

Charges on DNA and gel:

-DNA is negatively charged, so when it is placed in gel and is near an active positive charge, the DNA will seep through the gel and make its way towards the positive charge.

Heart blood movement: The Left Ventricle moves oxygenated blood, and The superior and inferior vena cava move deoxygenated blood. Blood comes into the right atrium from the body, moves into the right ventricle, and is pushed into the pulmonary arteries in the lungs. After picking up oxygen, the blood travels back to the heart through the pulmonary veins into the left atrium, to the left ventricle, and out to the body's tissues through the aorta. Pulmonary circulation moves blood between the heart and the lungs. It transports deoxygenated blood to the lungs to absorb oxygen and release carbon dioxide. The oxygenated blood then flows back to the heart. Systemic circulation moves blood between the heart and the rest of the body.

Ecology Unit

Ecology: the study of interactions between organisms and their environments;

This often includes an energy exchange.

Levels Of Organization:

Individual Organisms: The first, basic unit of organization. Habitat, life cycles, niches, and behavior can be studied at the individual level

Species: A species is a group of organisms that can reproduce and have fertile offspring.

Populations: A population is a group of individuals of the same species, living in the same area at the same time.

Communities: A community is a collection of different species populations that inhabit an area.

Ecosystems: An ecosystem includes the living and non-living features of an area.

Biosphere: The earth

Relationships:

Predation: Predator and prey relationship

Competition: When organisms fight over the same limited resources (i.e. food, water,

living space)

Mutualism: A partnership between two species in which both benefit

Commensalism: One species benefits but the other species is neither harmed nor helped

Parasitism: When organisms that live in or on another organism feed on it without

immediately killing it

Carbon Cycle: Plants absorb carbon from the atmosphere, and store it in their bodies. When plants die, decomposers break down the plant and release its carbon back into the atmosphere. Combustion, or the burning of a plant, also releases the plant’s carbon. Animals inhale oxygen and exhale carbon dioxide, adding more carbon to the atmosphere.

Nitrogen Cycle: The nitrogen cycle; provides materials needed to build proteins. Proteins are

required by living things to make structures such as muscles, bones, hair, blood, and even enzymes. Bacteria in soil take nitrogen (gas) from the atmosphere and convert it into

a liquid form of nitrogen that plants can use. This is called nitrogen fixation. Plants absorb nitrogen to make amino acids. Amino acids are monomers (building blocks) for proteins. Decomposers break down dead organisms and extract nitrogen from their bodies; plants use this nitrogen too.

Water Cycle: The water cycle is the continuous movement of water on, above, and below the surface of the Earth and through the Earth’s many organisms Plants absorb H2O from soil; consumers absorb it from food/drink. H2O goes to the atmosphere through transpiration (water leaving plants) & evaporation. Atmospheric H2O vapor condenses into clouds and falls back down as precipitation in the form of rain or snow

Roles of organisms (producers, decomposers) in cycling nutrients within an ecosystem

  • Producers introduce energy into an ecosystem by turning sunlight or chemicals into glucose, which consumers use.
  • Decomposers return energy to the ecosystem by breaking down dead organisms.

Importance of Biodiversity for ecosystem health:

  • Biodiversity is essential for the processes that support all life on Earth, including humans. Without a wide range of animals, plants, and microorganisms, we cannot have healthy ecosystems

How invasive species impact biodiversity:

  • Invasive species don’t have a natural predator, so their population grows out of control.
  • They can kill certain species too much, lowering biodiversity.

Positive feedback loop: A change in an ecosystem that causes an effect reinforcing the first change.

        For example, when polar ice caps melt, the ocean absorbs more heat, and the ice caps melt faster.

Negative feedback loop: A change in an ecosystem that causes an effect that balances the first change.

        Ex: Predator-prey relationship. If there are a lot of rabbits in one year, the wolves will have a lot of food and more children. This causes the wolves to eat more rabbits, causing the rabbit population to decline.

And their roles in maintaining homeostasis or the opposite:

  • Negative feedback loops maintain homeostasis by balancing the environment, while positive feedback loops throw it out of balance.

Factors that Affect Population Size: Births, Deaths, Immigration, Emigration

Conditions that cause a population to increase or decrease: If there is more immigration than emigration and more births than deaths then a population grows and if there is the opposite of that then a population decreases

Carrying Capacity: Carrying capacity is the largest population of a species that the environment can support (without damage to organisms or habitat) Carrying capacity has a limit. These are called limiting factors

Limiting Factors: Limiting factors restrict the growth of populations, Examples include: food & water supplies, predators, disease, etc.

Exponential Growth: Rapid growth that exceeds the carrying capacity

Logistic Growth: A population grows steadily because there are plenty of resources; it then slows and reaches a stable maximum curve

Boom and Bust Cycle: Also known as a population crash; population grows rapidly, exceeds the carrying capacity, then quickly declines

Predator Prey Cycle: Decreased predator population causes an increase in prey population and vice versa

How the green revolution and technological advancements have affected human carrying capacity:

  • Caused an increase in food production, which directly affects the world population.
  • Green revolution increased population, to the point where human population was over the carrying capacity

How human activities affect carrying capacity and biodiversity:

  • Every day, about 385,000 babies are born, and 150,000 people die. This uneven rate of deaths and births is a cause of our own actions, which will eventually bring us to a point where we are too far over the carrying capacity.
  • Human activities directly affect the number of species in various ecosystems with things such as hunting for certain species of animals, or our deforestation, which causes the death of lots of important trees/organisms in an ecosystem, directly affecting all of it’s organisms.

Are humans living above or below earth’s carrying capacity, considering environmental impacts?

  • We are way above our carrying capacity as we are using more resources than the earth is able to create for us and eventually we will run out of natural gas, oil, coal, etc.
